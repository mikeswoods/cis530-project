AROUND 6:30 most weekday mornings, a motherly looking woman in a suit and spectacles catches a commuter bus with her teen-age daughter in Flushing, Queens. As the pair trundle along their 40-minute journey to their destinations in lower Manhattan, an office and a high school, they share the newspaper, do the crossword puzzle and chat about their plans for the day.
But as she rides the elevator to the 16th floor of a tall brick tower near Wall Street, this ordinary-looking woman stands revealed as someone quite different: the muse of the bull market, whose pronouncements can send stocks soaring or reeling.
